01. Problem Discovery

From assumption to validated insight.

I ran a two-week self-audit on my own job search, logging every session, every task, every minute, to find the real Job-to-be-Done before writing a line of code.

Scope of this case study
This is a solo project with a sample size of one: me. Every insight below comes from my own tracked behavior, not a multi-person research study, and I've kept the language honest to that scope rather than dressing it up as broader user research. What this project does demonstrate: discovery discipline, trade-off reasoning under real constraints, and systems thinking carried from insight to shipped architecture. What it doesn't demonstrate: stakeholder negotiation or cross-functional influence. I'm glad to walk through examples of that from my day job separately.
The Job-to-be-Done (as I experienced it)
  • "Help me identify the right opportunities without spending hours on coordination"
  • "Help me craft tailored applications that actually get responses"
  • "Help me stay consistent without burning out on repetitive tasks"
Hypothesis 1: Invalidated

"I need to apply to more jobs."

I started with the classic volume hypothesis: job searching is a numbers game. I optimized for throughput: more applications, more platforms, more tabs. The result was burnout, generic applications, and a 0% response rate on my spray-and-pray submissions (12 applications, 0 replies, over 4 days). The problem wasn't effort; it was signal-to-noise ratio.

Hypothesis 2: Invalidated

"I need a better tracker."

Next, I built a spreadsheet to organize my pipeline. It helped with visibility but created a new coordination tax: manual data entry, stale statuses, and decision fatigue about what to prioritize each morning. The tool was solving organization, not the underlying friction.

Insight: Validated

"It's a systemic coordination problem."

I logged every session over two weeks and categorized every block of time by task type. The split held steady across both weeks: roughly 70% of my time went to coordination: finding roles, cross-referencing requirements, tailoring documents, tracking follow-ups. Only about 30% went to strategic work: researching companies, preparing for interviews, building relationships. The insight: the best candidates don't have more discipline; they have better systems that eliminate coordination work entirely.

Key Product Insight
The opportunity wasn't optimizing coordination. It was eliminating it through intelligent automation. The product wedge was a daily briefing that could replace roughly three hours of manual work with a three-minute review.

02. Product Decisions

Every feature had to earn its place.

A decision log for the five choices that shaped the product: what I picked, what I rejected, and what would make me reverse each call.

1 · Daily email brief, not a dashboard, not full auto-apply
Alternatives considered
A pull-based dashboard I'd check manually; full auto-apply with no review step.
Why this one
Both alternatives failed in testing (see Section 04). A dashboard added a surface to check rather than removing work; full automation removed my ability to vouch for what was sent under my name.
What would change my mind
Evidence that I'd check a dashboard proactively without a push nudge, or a scoring model reliable enough that review stops adding signal.
2 · ATS score threshold of 80 as the "qualified" bar
Alternatives considered
70 (more volume, more noise) and 90 (higher precision, fewer roles surfaced, closer to 15% of scraped postings than 65%).
Why this one
I manually re-read ~20 borderline-scored postings and found 80 was where the scorer's judgment matched mine most consistently. Below that threshold, false positives crept in fast.
What would change my mind
False-positive/negative rates measured at a scale bigger than one person's search. My current sample is too small to tune this with real confidence.
3 · Human approval gate between Tailor and Apply
Alternatives considered
Fully autonomous apply; an approval step earlier, before tailoring, instead of right before send.
Why this one
Placing the gate right before send meant I was reviewing the actual artifact going out, not an intermediate draft: the highest-leverage place to catch a bad tailoring pass.
What would change my mind
Demonstrated tailoring reliability over a much larger sample than I could generate solo.
4 · GitHub Actions over custom infrastructure
Alternatives considered
AWS Lambda + EventBridge; a small always-on VPS with cron.
Why this one
Zero infrastructure maintenance, built-in logs, and free at my usage volume. The product's value was in the scoring and tailoring logic, not the scheduler, so I optimized for shipping speed there.
What would change my mind
A need for sub-minute scheduling precision, or handling data sensitive enough that I wanted it off a third-party CI runner.
5 · Parallel AM / PM pipelines over a single daily batch
Alternatives considered
Single morning batch; real-time polling of job-board APIs.
Why this one
Job postings have a short half-life and job boards update through the day. Real-time polling meant building a streaming system for marginal benefit; two scheduled runs captured most of the gain for a fraction of the engineering cost.
What would change my mind
Job boards offering webhooks or push notifications, which would make polling of any kind unnecessary.

The pipeline enforces a human approval gate between "Tailor" and "Apply": a deliberate product decision to protect quality control and my own trust in the system over pure throughput. See Decision #3 above for the full trade-off.
GitHub Actions: Orchestration Layer The parallel workflow architecture (morning + afternoon runs) emerged from a product constraint: job boards update throughout the day. Rather than one batch job, the system runs two pipelines to capture fresh postings. Every step is logged, timed, and recoverable. The run history above is real output from this project, not a mockup.
Technical Choice: GitHub Actions over Custom Infrastructure
Product reasoning: Using GitHub Actions meant zero infrastructure maintenance, built-in observability, and free scheduling. The trade-off was less control over the execution environment, which was acceptable because the product value lived in the intelligence layer, not the orchestration layer.
Technical Choice: Parallel Pipelines over Single Batch
Product reasoning: Job postings have a half-life. A morning-only pipeline missed afternoon updates from ATS APIs. Over the first two weeks of each configuration, the morning-only pipeline surfaced roughly 1.8 qualified roles/day; adding the afternoon run brought that to roughly 2.5/day, an estimated 40% lift. It's measured on a small sample and not adjusted for week-to-week posting volume, so I'd treat it as directional rather than precise.
04. Product Solution

Daily intelligent briefings.

The digest email became the activation hook, not because it automated work, but because it replaced decision fatigue with clarity.

Invalidated
Full automation, no review
Auto-apply without review created anxiety about what was going out under my name, and produced a handful of low-quality submissions in the first week I tried it. I wanted control, not abdication.
Invalidated
A dashboard to check
A dashboard added another surface to check rather than removing a task. I kept forgetting to open it. I wanted something pushed to me, not something to remember to pull.
Validated
A daily briefing
A single email with qualified opportunities, tailored suggestions, and clear next actions reduced cognitive load enough that I actually acted on it every morning.
The Daily Brief: Product Surface Every morning, a personalized briefing showing qualified opportunities (ATS 80+), a pipeline health score, tailored resume suggestions, and specific follow-up recommendations. This replaced roughly 3 hours of manual coordination with a 3-minute review. The screenshot above is the actual product, not a recreation.

06. If CareerOS Were a Company

Market thinking, not just build thinking.

This was built for an audience of one, but the discipline of asking "would this work as a business" is worth doing anyway, and it surfaces a real tension worth naming.

Customer
Active job seekers doing a high-volume, time-boxed search: people applying to 15+ roles a month, not passive browsers. That's a narrower ICP than "anyone job hunting," and it matters, because the product's value is proportional to search intensity.
Pricing
A monthly subscription is the wrong shape for this. Job searching is episodic: a search sprint pass (an eight-week active-search pass, say) fits actual usage better than a recurring charge someone forgets to cancel once they're hired.
Go-to-market
Community-led, not paid acquisition: job-search communities (r/jobs-style forums), LinkedIn content built transparently around the build-in-public process, and referral from people who got hired using it, which is the strongest possible testimonial.
Expansion
Not seat expansion: win-back. The same person returns for their next search, one to four years later. The product should be built to be easy to reactivate, not to retain daily.
The real business tension
This product's best possible outcome (the user gets hired) ends their need for it. A standard SaaS retention/DAU north star actively fights the user's interest here. The honest north star for a company built on this is something like time-to-hire or hire rate per active search, with revenue coming from sprint passes and referral-driven new sprints, not from maximizing time spent in the product. Recognizing that a product's success metric and its business model can pull in opposite directions is, to me, the most valuable insight in this whole exercise, more so than any single feature decision above.

Closing Reflection
CareerOS started as an automation project.
It became a lesson in product thinking, and in the limits of a sample size of one.
"What I built matters less than what I learned about problem discovery, decision-making under constraint, and being honest about what n=1 evidence can and can't prove."
1
Validate before building. The cost of being wrong is always higher than the cost of learning. Two invalidated hypotheses, tested cheaply, saved weeks of misdirected engineering on the third.
2
Constraints create clarity. Technical limitations (GitHub Actions, no dedicated infra) shaped a better architecture than unlimited resources would have.
3
Human judgment is a feature, not a bug. The approval gate wasn't a limitation. It was the reason I trusted the system enough to rely on it every day.
4
Systems > tools. A tool you use once is a novelty. A system that learns and improves with every use creates compound returns.
5
Small-N evidence is still evidence, if you label it honestly. Every number in this case study comes from one person's search. That's a real limitation, not a footnote to hide. Saying so up front made every other claim here more credible, not less. It's the discipline I'd bring to interpreting any dataset, at any size.
